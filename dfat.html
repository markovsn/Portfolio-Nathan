Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade

Website redesign

Challenge

The DFAT website is a complicated repository of information related to Australia's foreign affairs and trade. Poor information hierarchy and architecture are diminishing the user experience, as finding information is tedious and time consuming.

Solution

Redesigning the information architecture of the navigation bar to make it easier for users to find the information they need. Updating the interface design of the homepage to reduce visual noise and re-priortise key sections.

My role

Sole UX/UI Designer

Timing

Three weeks

Research

Website evaluation

An initial scan of the website found a number of issues including:

  • large navigation dropdown menus with overwhelming amounts of text,
  • high levels of visual noise content is scattered across the page without effective use of grids or spacing,
  • dull use of colours that breached W3C colour accessibility standards.

    • User Testing

    Five user tests were conducted to determine how quickly and effectively users could navigate across the website.

    Users found navigation frustrating and confusing by the general naming of the nav links and large dropdowns. Users took notable time to scan through pages due to the volume of content and poor information hierarchy.

    How might improve the navigation so that users can efficiently find the information they are searching for?

    Information architecture

    Card sorting

    I conducted a virtual card sorting activity with two participants by using the titles of the primary and secondary pages in the original navigation bar. Participants were free to arrange the cards in the existing categories or create their own.

    Site map

    Participants in the card sorting noted large amounts of topics that could be captured under a broader categories. Therefore, I condensed three previous nav categories into one broader category of "Policy Issues".

    The new site map was informed by the results of the testing and card sorting. Primary nav categories were reduced from a total of seven to four. Sub categories were organised in alphabetical order to improve the speed of navigation.

    Competitor analysis

    I analysed the key features from the website homepages of several domestic and international government agencies with a similar focus as DFAT.

    I noticed most of these websites were designed with simplicity in mind. They had plenty of white space, navigation bars were simply with few links, and information was organised discrete containers and sections.

    Building

    Sketching and wireframing

    I sketched out the design of the homepage with the idea of keeping things simple and structured. Headings were used to indicate seperation between sections. Cards were used to chunk information for improved readability. A 12 column grid was applied to the digital wireframes to ensure consistent spacing.

    Testing

    I conducted two rounds of testing to determine how quickly users could complete several navigation tasks. I iterated the navigation bar based according to the findings.

    For example, users found the initial navigation dropdown quite large. I focused on reducing its size in proceeding versions.

    Responsive design

    A 12 column grid was applied to all screens. Breakpoints were applied at ‘max-width: 950px’ for tablet and ‘max-width: 450px’ for mobile.

    The navigation bar was hidden behind a burger menu from tablet mode. The search icon remained viewable in tablet and mobile as it was widely used by users to find information in the initial testing.

    Style Guide

    I developed a style guide to showcase the key branding elements for the app design. The branding takes a 'hand drawn' effect with the abundant use of white and black outlines. Bright colours are used sparringly to signify a fun and energy.

    Final design

    Homepage

    The homepage was redesigned using cards and containers to provide structure and reduce visual noise. Sections were reordered based on user needs. A collapsable section for emergency contacts appears first, followed by news and updates from the Department.

    Countries and regions - European Union

    Navigation

    A horizontal navigation bar was maintained to save space on the homepage and provide more room for the dropdowns. Nav links were condensed into four broad categories to reduce choice overload.
